About the Jathere

A seat of ancestors, kept alive by memory.

“जिथे जठेरे, उथे जड़ां।” Where the elders rest, there our roots remain. — Pahari saying

In the highlands of North India and the plains of Jammu, a Jathera is far more than a shrine. It is the living covenant between a clan and the forebears who first cleared the land, raised the first hearth, and gave the family its name. To visit the Jathere is to stand at the source of one's own bloodline — to thank the vadde-vaddere, the great elders, whose courage made every descendant possible.

For the Johal Vansh — a Jat clan of the Agnash gotra — the Jathere at Bhojpur is exactly such a sacred origin. Here the clan venerates its Kul Devta — the ancestral guardian-deity — through offerings at the harvest, before weddings, and at the birth of every child. By Pahari custom a newly-married couple and a newborn are brought first to the Jathere, so that the elders may bless the new branch before the world ever sees it. It is devotion not to a distant god, but to one's own people made eternal.

The tradition asks little and gives much: a lamp lit in remembrance, a few grains offered in gratitude, foreheads bowed in a circle of kin. In that quiet act the Johal family renews a promise thousands of years old — that no ancestor is forgotten, and no descendant stands alone. This page exists to keep that promise reachable, wherever in the world a Johal may now live.

History & Lineage · इतिहास व वंशावली

An ancient name, carried across the north-west.

Long before the shrine at Bhojpur, the Johal were known across the frontier — a people whose very name carried defiance. Drawn from the tribal genealogies (kursinama) and from published histories, this is the story of the Johal Jatt tribe and its kindred line, the Jhalli.

The Johal · जौहल / जहल

The name Johal — also written Jahal, Yohal or Jauhal — is traditionally understood to mean a rebel, a hawk, one who is defiant and will not bow before an adversary. The Johal are counted among the ancient Jatt tribes of the Surajbansi line — the Sun dynasty — recognised since before the Common Era in the old Aryan Jatt country (Jatkistan) toward Sindh. Across the centuries the tribe has spread far, and today counts Hindus, Muslims and Sikhs among its descendants.

In the books

The tribe's antiquity is supported by two well-known works:

Jats: The Ancient Rulers
B. S. Dahiya

Records that the name "Jaul" or "Jauhal" is inscribed on ancient coins and monuments of rulers such as Toramana and Mihirakula. It notes that Arab chroniclers, unable to pronounce "Johal", rendered it "Jabal" — so the Johal-populated country around Kabul and Ghazni was set down in history as "Jabalistan" rather than "Johalistan". Coins of an ancient Johal king named Vikbah have also been found.

A Glossary of the Tribes & Castes
E. D. Maclagan & H. A. Rose

This survey of the Punjab and North-West Frontier records the Johals as a small agricultural Jatt tribe of the Amritsar district — a documentary trace of the family's long settlement in the land of the five rivers.

The lineage · वंशावली

By the genealogies, the Johal line descends through a chain of remembered forebears:

  • Mool Raj · मूल राजhead of the line; father of Chalak Raj
  • Sotrak · सोत्रकson of Chalak Raj — credited by tradition with founding the ancient university at Taxila
  • Saroa (Saroia) · सरोआson of Sotrak
  • Hanjra · हंजराson of Saroa; father of Johal
  • Johal · जौहलthe eponymous ancestor of the tribe

Johal's brothers were said to be Mohal, Hundal, Sohal, Hujjan, Hans and Kashkol — the name Hans is held to echo even in the German "Hanz".

Valour & settlement

For centuries the brave men of the Johal are remembered as guardians of the Khyber Pass, holding the gateway against Arab invaders, and as builders of the Qila Johal — the Johal Fort — near Peshawar.

Today the tribe is settled across Sindh, Punjab and Haryana. In the Jammu region — where the name is pronounced "Jahal" — the Johal number over a thousand across Kathua, Samba and Poonch, with the Jathere at Bhojpur as their ancestral seat.

A kindred tribe · the Jhalli

The Jhalli, an Aganbans (Fire-dynasty) Jatt tribe, share the same deep ancestral tree — the Aganbans line itself springing from four sons of Mool Raj associated with fire: Parmar, Bhadiar, Chalok (Solangi) and Chauhan. Their name is traditionally read as "comfort-giver" — from the great cloth ceiling-fans (jhal) once pulled by hand to cool a hall and to carry a speaker's voice across the gathering. Maclagan & Rose recorded them as a small Jatt tribe of Ambala, but mistook the meaning for "kamla" (mad), missing the true sense of the word. Like the Johal, the Jhalli today include Hindus, Muslims and Sikhs.

Tradition traces the Jhalli from Hazrat Adam (named in Hindi tradition as Shiv Shankar / Mahadev) through Hazrat Nooh (Noah, the second Manu). After the great flood, Nooh's son Sam settled in Asia; Sam's son Arfakhasind (Sindh) fathered the line whose descendants raised the great cities — Gara founding Harappa, another Taxila, and Moola founding Moolastan (Multan). Moola's line gave Iram, Aad (linked to Mohenjo-daro), Jalud and Bah; Bah's son Gorakh (Hazrat Hood); and generations on, Mool Raj, whose son Chalok (Solangi) led to Maidan, then Kalwar — and Kalwar's son was Jhalli.

Jhalli's sons — Kirwal, Jabeel, Lale (Lalia), Soonda and Sarae — founded the sub-branches. The tribe is widely dispersed today: in Pakistan across Sindh (Shikarpur, Thatta, Badin, Nawabshah, Hyderabad) and Punjab (Hasan Abdal, Fatehpur, Layyah, Jhang, Sargodha, Multan, Bahawalpur); in India around Delhi and East Punjab; and as far as Zahedan, Iran. Some of the tribe historically held the respected title of Qazi.

This account is drawn from the tribal genealogies (kursinama / cursinama) preserved within the family and from the published works named above. Portions reflect oral tradition and genealogical memory rather than documentary record, and are shared here in reverence, to honour the lineage and keep it within reach of every descendant.

  • लड़के का जन्म हो तो 12 दिन तक एक छत के नीचे सोना और तारों की छांव में बाहर निकलना चाहिए।

Visit & Location

Finding the Jathere at Bhojpur.

The shrine sits in the village belt of Bhojpur, within the Ranbir Singh Pura (R. S. Pura) tehsil of Jammu district — the fertile basmati country just south of Jammu city, near the international border.
